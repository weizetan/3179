Redefining
HAPPINESS in 2022

Welcome to the 2022 World Happiness Report. This report explores happiness worldwide. It looks at how happy different countries are and why. We collect data from 146 countries to understand what makes people happy.
We measure happiness on a scale from 1 to 10, where 1 represents the unhappiest and 10 the happiest. This measurement is based on factors like money, support from others, health, freedom, fairness, and kindness. Governments and organizations use this information to make decisions that can improve people's lives. So, let's dive into the happiness of the world in 2022!

It's interesting to observe that Finland leads among those countries with the highest happiness score, indicating that its residents enjoy more overall wellbeing and life satisfaction than those in other nations throughout the world.

We have identified six key factors that contribute to Happiness Score :

1. GDP per capita
2. Social Support
3. Healthy Life Expectancy
4. Freedom of Life Choices
5. Generosity
6. Perceptions of Corruption

These factors play a significant role in determining a country's overall happiness, with economic well-being, social support, health, freedom, generosity, and trust all contributing to the overall happiness level.

By carefully examining the graph above, we can see that GDP per capita is within the greatest range, indicating that it has a considerable impact on how the happiness score is determined.

We can observe that the first four factors (GDP per capita, Social Support, Healthy Life Expectancy, and Freedom of Life Choices) exhibit a similar data pattern. All the data points for these factors are situated at the higher end of their respective trend lines, indicating a strong positive correlation. In simpler terms, when these factors are higher, the happiness score tends to be higher as well.

On the other hand, Perceptions of Corruption stands out from these four factors. Its data points are located at the lower end of the trend line, suggesting a negative correlation. In other words, when perceptions of corruption are lower, happiness scores tend to be higher.

For Generosity, the trend line is nearly horizontal, indicating a weak correlation with happiness scores. In simple terms, there isn't a strong connection between generosity and happiness scores based on this data.
